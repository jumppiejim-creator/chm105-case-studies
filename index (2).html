The Missing Trophy

Case File #105-01  ·  Chapter 1: Forensic Science & Observation

Dispatch Report

Monday morning. A routine check becomes anything but routine.

⏱ Monday, 7:45 AM — Riverside Community College

Campus security officer Marcus Chen arrives at the athletic department to begin his routine check of the facilities. As he walks past the trophy case in the main hallway, something immediately catches his attention — the center display is empty.

The college's prized National Championship trophy from the 1995 women's basketball team is missing. The trophy, valued at $8,500, has been a centerpiece of the school's athletic pride for nearly three decades.

The glass door of the trophy case is slightly ajar, and Marcus notices that the alarm system panel near the case is showing a green light, indicating it was never armed the previous evening.

⏱ Monday, 8:05 AM — Detective Arrives

Marcus immediately calls the local police department, and Detective Sarah Williams arrives within twenty minutes. As she approaches the scene, she knows that her role will require careful observation and systematic investigation.

Before she examines anything, Detective Williams pauses and mentally reviews the principles of forensic science she relies on every day.

Case Report

The investigation concludes. Review the final report and reflect on the lessons learned.

Case Resolution

The case concludes with no criminal charges filed. Coach Stevens receives a formal warning about proper procedures for removing items from displays, and the athletic department implements a new policy requiring written authorization for any removal of trophies or memorabilia.

Detective Williams's Final Report (CER Model)

Claim

Assistant Coach Mike Stevens removed the championship trophy from the display case on Sunday, January 15th, between 7:25 PM and 7:55 PM for use at a scheduled alumni reunion event.

Evidence

  • Digital access logs confirm Coach Stevens entered the building at 7:25 PM and exited at 7:55 PM
  • Witness Jamie Rodriguez observed the trophy was missing at approximately 8:00 PM
  • The trophy was recovered from Coach Stevens's office
  • Multiple witnesses confirmed the trophy was present at the alumni dinner at the Riverside Hotel
  • Hotel records confirm the event booking and date

Reasoning

The timeline established by witness statements and access logs, combined with the physical recovery of the trophy and corroboration of the alumni event, supports the conclusion that Coach Stevens removed the trophy for legitimate purposes. No evidence of criminal intent was found.

Lessons from the Field

Detective Williams takes a moment to debrief with Marcus Chen. She explains that while his initial suspicion of David Martinez was understandable, it was based on perception rather than observation.

"The key to good forensic investigation," Detective Williams explains, "is to delay interpretation and focus on collecting facts first. I had to act like a robot — gathering data systematically without letting my mental filters decide what was important too early. Once I had all the observations documented and the witness statements recorded, only then could I analyze what really happened."

Marcus nods thoughtfully. "I see what you mean. I jumped to a conclusion based on my feeling that David had been 'acting suspicious,' but I never stopped to think about what specific behaviors I had actually observed or whether those behaviors actually meant anything suspicious at all."
